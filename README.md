# CS255
-Briefly summarize the DriverPass project. Who was the client? What type of system did they want you to design?
--The DriverPass project was based on creating a system for a company that provides driver training services. The client was the owner of DriverPass, who was concerned about the high number of students failing their driving exams. He wanted a system that would better prepare students through structured online and in-person training. The system we were asked to design was a web-based platform where students could create accounts, enroll in driving packages, complete online coursework and practice exams, and schedule behind-the-wheel lessons. The system also needed administrative features so the owner and staff could manage student records, schedules, payments, and reports.

-What did you do particularly well?
--I think I did a strong job identifying and organizing the system requirements. I made sure to separate functional and non-functional requirements clearly and tie them back to what the client actually asked for. I also focused on making the UML diagrams consistent with the written requirements so everything connected logically. Making sure the diagrams reflected the real workflow of students, instructors, and administrators was something I paid close attention to.

-If you could choose one part of your work on these documents to revise, what would you pick? How would you improve it?
--If I could revise one section, I would improve the non-functional requirements. While I included performance, security, and usability requirements, I think I could make them more specific and measurable. Instead of general statements, I would define clearer benchmarks, such as response times, system uptime percentages, and security standards. I would also gather more direct feedback from potential users to make sure those requirements reflect real-world expectations.

-How did you interpret the user’s needs and implement them into your system design? Why is it so important to consider the user’s needs when designing?
--I interpreted the user’s needs by carefully reviewing the interview transcript and identifying the main concerns of the client. The owner wanted better student preparation, easier scheduling, and organized record management. I translated those needs into features like online practice exams, lesson scheduling tools, account management, and reporting capabilities. Considering the user’s needs is critical because the system ultimately exists to solve their problems. If the design does not match what users actually need, even a technically well-built system can fail. Keeping the user at the center of the design ensures the system is practical, usable, and valuable.

-How do you approach designing software? What techniques or strategies would you use in the future to analyze and design a system?
--When designing software, I start by understanding the problem before thinking about the solution. I break down requirements, identify stakeholders, and map out major system processes. I use techniques like use case diagrams, activity diagrams, and class diagrams to visualize how the system should function. In the future, I would also use more prototyping and user feedback early in the design phase. Creating mockups or wireframes and reviewing them with stakeholders could help catch misunderstandings before development begins. Overall, I try to approach system design in a structured and organized way so that each part builds logically on the previous one.
